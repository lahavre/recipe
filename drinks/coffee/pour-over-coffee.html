Pour-Over Coffee

A precise, timed four-pour method — the first pour sets acidity, the second builds sweetness, and the last two dial in the concentration. About two and a half minutes start to finish.

14g : 200g (~1:14 ratio) 91°C water Coarse grind ~2:30 total
1 cup

    Method

    1. Prep your gear Rinse the paper filter in your dripper with hot water to remove any papery taste, and warm the server — then discard that water. Add the ground coffee to the filter and give it a gentle shake to level the bed.
    2. First pour — sets the acidity Start your timer and pour the first 20% of your water (40g for a 200g brew) over the grounds, spending about 10 seconds on the pour. Agitate the dripper gently 2–3 times.
      Until next pour 0:40
    3. Second pour — builds the sweetness At 0:40, pour steadily and a little more aggressively up to 100g total (another 20% of your water), again over about 10 seconds.
      Until next pour 0:30
    4. Third pour — adjusts concentration At 1:10, pour gently up to 150g total (25% of your water) to fine-tune the strength — heavier here makes it stronger, lighter makes it milder.
      Until next pour 0:30
    5. Fourth pour — finishes the brew At 1:40, pour the final 50g gently up to 200g total, then give the dripper a final gentle stir or swirl to even out the extraction.
      Until drawdown ends 0:50
    6. Finish and serve Once the water fully drains from the coffee bed — around 2:30 total — remove the dripper and serve right away.

    Good to know

    • Spend about 10 seconds on each individual pour for consistency — pouring too fast or too slow changes how evenly the water saturates the grounds.
    • The first pour is mostly about acidity, the second about sweetness, and the third and fourth are where you dial in the concentration to taste.
    • A coarse grind and a light roast are recommended for this method. If your brew runs too fast, grind a touch finer; if it runs too slow, go coarser.
    • If you scale up to a bigger batch, keep the same four-pour rhythm and proportions — the roughly 1:14 coffee-to-water ratio is what matters most, not the exact gram figures.
