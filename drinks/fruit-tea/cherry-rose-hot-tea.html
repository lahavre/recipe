Cherry Rose Hot Tea

A warm, ruby-red infusion of hibiscus and rose with blackcurrant and peach — steeped just off the boil, sweetened while hot, and served warm (or poured over ice).

    Method

    1. Boil the water Bring fresh water to a boil, then let it settle for a few seconds so it's just off the boil. Warm your teapot or cup with a little of it if you like — it keeps the brew hotter for longer.
    2. Add the tea Put the cherry rose tea into the pot or a cup (or an infuser basket). Add the dried rose buds now too if using — the few minutes of hot steeping draws the rose out gently. Break up any large peach or fruit chunks so they extract evenly.
    3. Pour, cover, and steep Pour the hot water over the tea, cover to hold the heat in, and steep 5–8 minutes. There are no real tea leaves here, so it won't turn bitter — longer just means deeper and more tart. Start at 6 minutes and adjust to taste.
      Steep 6:00
    4. Add fresh fruit (optional) Drop in a few thin peach slices or an orange/lemon slice while it steeps — the heat pulls their flavour out quickly. No need for the "add citrus late" trick the cold brew uses; the steep is minutes, not hours, so the peel won't turn bitter.
    5. Sweeten while it's hot This blend runs properly tart, so it usually wants sweetening — and hot is the easy time to do it. Stir in rock sugar (3–4 pieces per 500ml) or a spoonful of honey while the brew is hot; both dissolve instantly, so there's no syrup step like the cold brew needs. Taste and adjust.
    6. Strain and serve Strain into cups through a fine sieve (or lift out the infuser). Serve hot, dropping a fresh peach or citrus slice into each cup. Or sweeten it a touch stronger and pour over ice for a quick iced version — it's lovely chilled.

    Good to know

    • No real tea leaves means no tannins — it won't go bitter, so steep time is purely about strength and tartness, not avoiding a harsh cup.
    • Hibiscus (洛神花) plus blackcurrant makes this the tart one. Plan on sweetening, and lean toward the 5-minute steep if you're sour-sensitive.
    • Hot brewing is the easy way to sweeten — rock sugar and honey dissolve instantly, unlike in cold brew where they need to be made into a syrup first.
    • The dried rose is already in the blend; adding a few buds just nudges the floral note up. A small pinch is plenty — too much reads soapy.
    • The coconut jelly (椰果) won't really brew — it just rehydrates and settles. Scoop a few pieces into your cup to chew if you like.
    • Best fresh and hot. Leftovers keep in the fridge 1–2 days; gently rewarm, or pour over ice for an iced cup.
