Forest Berry Cold Brew

A tart, ruby-red berry-hibiscus infusion with fresh blueberries, orange, and apple — slow-steeped cold in a 1L pitcher and strained over ice.

1L pitcher or jar 6–12 h steep Caffeine-free Best served cold
4 glasses

    Method

    1. Add the tea Add the Forest Berry tea straight into your pitcher or jar. Break up any large dried strawberry or apple pieces so they soak evenly.
    2. Add the apple and blueberries Slice the apple into thin slices (skin on, washed) and add to the bottle along with the blueberries. Lightly press a few blueberries against the side to release juice.
    3. Fill with cold water Pour in 1L of cold filtered water. The tea and fruit will float and steep freely in the water.
    4. Swirl, cap, and chill Give it a gentle swirl so everything is wetted, cover with a lid, and refrigerate. This blend is hibiscus-heavy, so it runs tart: 6 hours gives a lighter, pinker cup; overnight gives a fuller, deeper, tart-sweet result. Swirl once midway to keep the fruit submerged.
      Steep 8:00:00
    5. Add the orange near the end Add the thinly sliced orange in the last 2–3 hours of steeping, so the peel doesn't turn bitter over the full brew.
      Orange in 2:30:00
    6. Sweeten to taste (optional) Taste first — but this blend is properly tart, so it usually wants a little sweetening. Dissolve rock sugar or honey in a splash of warm water to make a syrup, cool it, then stir it in (cold liquid won't dissolve either one well). Or sweeten by the glass so the batch keeps longer.
    7. Pour and serve Strain through a fine sieve into a glass of ice. The sieve holds back the berries and petals for a clean, ruby-red cup.

    Good to know

    • Drink within 1–2 days since it contains fresh fruit — give it a sniff before drinking on day 2.
    • Muddled blueberries will make it slightly cloudy; still tastes great.
    • If the hibiscus tartness is too sharp, lean toward the shorter 6-hour steep or add a touch more sweetener.
    • The orange and apple both soften the tart edge nicely — taste before adding extra sugar.
