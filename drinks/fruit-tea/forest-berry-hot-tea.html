Forest Berry Hot Tea

The Forest Berry berry-hibiscus blend brewed warm — apple, orange, and grape simmered into a fruit base, with passion fruit and blueberries stirred in off the heat. Ruby-red and gently tart.

Stovetop · ~1L pot ~12 min total Caffeine-free Best served hot

    Method

    1. Prep the fruit Slice the apple and orange thin (skin on, washed). Halve the grapes so they actually release their juice — whole grapes barely infuse. Halve the passion fruit and scoop the pulp and seeds into a bowl. Rinse the blueberries and lightly crush a few to deepen the colour.
    2. Build the fruit base Put the apple, orange, and halved grapes into a pot with the water. Bring to a gentle simmer and let it bubble softly for about 5 minutes to draw out the firm fruit. Don't add the Forest Berry tea yet.
      Simmer 5:00
    3. Off the heat, add the delicate fruit Take the pot off the heat. Stir in the passion fruit pulp and the blueberries. Boiling these would dull their fresh, bright character, so they go in once the simmer is done.
    4. Add the Forest Berry tea and steep Stir in the Forest Berry blend and steep off the heat. This is a hibiscus blend, so it runs sharper the hotter and longer it sits — 3 min gives a brighter, lighter cup; 5 min a fuller, deeper, more sour one. Keep it short if you're sour-sensitive.
      Steep 4:00
    5. Strain and sweeten Strain into a warmed teapot or straight into cups through a fine sieve. This blend is properly tart, so it usually wants sweetening — stir in honey or rock sugar while it's hot, where it dissolves instantly (no syrup step needed, unlike the cold brews). Taste and adjust.
    6. Serve hot Pour into cups. Drop a fresh blueberry, a grape half, or an orange slice into each cup — pretty to look at, and it keeps gently flavouring the tea as you sip.

    Good to know

    • Hibiscus heated runs sharp. Forest Berry is a berry-hibiscus blend, so brewing it hot brings out more tartness than the cold steep does. Keep the steep short and plan on sweetening — that's the main trick to a balanced hot cup.
    • Halve the grapes. Whole grapes barely give anything to the pot; cut they add a soft, juicy sweetness that rounds off the passion-fruit and hibiscus tartness nicely.
    • Passion fruit and blueberries go off the heat — simmering dulls their bright, fresh top notes. The apple, orange, and grape are the ones that benefit from the gentle simmer.
    • Skip lemon or lime here. On an already tart blend it's just more acid; the orange brightens it enough.
    • Passion fruit seeds are edible and look beautiful floating in the cup — strain them out if you prefer it smooth.
    • Best fresh and hot. Leftovers keep in the fridge 1–2 days; gently rewarm, or pour over ice for an impromptu iced version.
