Osmanthus Jasmine Cold Brew

A soft, golden floral-fruit infusion of apricot-sweet osmanthus and jasmine over dried tropical fruit and rose — slow-steeped cold in a 1L pitcher and strained over ice.

1L pitcher or jar 6–12 h steep Caffeine-free Best served cold
Batch 4 glasses
1 L

Ingredients

    Method

    1. Add the tea Add the osmanthus jasmine tea straight into your pitcher or jar. Break up any large dried fruit chunks so they soak evenly.
    2. Add the fresh fruit Slice about half an apple into 6–8 thin slices (skin on, washed) and drop them in. Add a few thin lemon slices too if you'd like a brighter lift — it also keeps the apple from browning.
    3. Fill with cold water Pour in 1L of cold filtered water. The tea, flowers, and fruit will float and steep freely in the water.
    4. Swirl, cap, and chill Give it a gentle swirl so everything is wetted, cover with a lid, and refrigerate. 6 hours gives a light, delicate cup; overnight gives a fuller, sweeter one. Swirl once midway so any floating apple stays submerged.
      08:00:00
    5. Sweeten to taste (optional) Taste first — between the dried fruit and apple it may not need anything. If you'd like it sweeter, dissolve 1–2 tsp honey or rock sugar in a splash of warm water to make a syrup, then stir it in. Adding it dry to cold liquid just sinks and clumps.
    6. Pour and serve Strain through a fine sieve into a glass of ice. The sieve holds back the fruit and petals for a clean, golden cup.

    Good to know

    • Straining only happens when you pour — all the steeping happens loose in the pitcher.
    • Unlike real tea leaves, this fruit-and-flower blend won't turn bitter if it sits, just gradually fuller. To stop it, strain the whole batch into another container.
    • With fresh apple in it, drink within 1–2 days and give it a sniff before drinking on day 2.
    • Half an apple (6–8 thin slices) is right for 1L — enough to round out the cup without burying the delicate osmanthus and jasmine.
    • Rock sugar is cleaner and more neutral; honey adds its own floral warmth. Either way, make it a syrup first.
