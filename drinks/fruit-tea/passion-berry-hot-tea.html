Passion Berry Hot Tea

A warm, sunset-coloured infusion of passion fruit and mixed berries with apple and orange over a black-tea base — gently simmered, steeped off the heat, and served hot.

Stovetop · ~1L pot ~10 min total Black, green, or herbal base Best served hot

    Method

    1. Prep the fruit Slice the apple and orange thin (skin on, washed). Halve the passion fruit and scoop the pulp and seeds into a bowl. Rinse the berries; halve any strawberries and lightly crush a few raspberries or blueberries to release their juice.
    2. Build the fruit base Put the apple and orange slices into a pot with the water (add the cinnamon stick or ginger now if using). Bring to a gentle simmer and let it bubble softly for about 5 minutes to draw out the apple and orange.
      Simmer 5:00
    3. Off the heat, add the delicate fruit Take the pot off the heat. Stir in the passion fruit pulp and the berries. Boiling these would dull their fresh, bright character, so they go in once the simmer is done.
    4. Add the tea and steep Add the tea leaves (or bags) and steep off the heat. Black tea: 3–5 min. Green tea: let the water cool a minute first, then steep only 2–3 min so it doesn't turn bitter. Don't boil the leaves.
      Steep 4:00
    5. Strain and sweeten Strain into a warmed teapot or straight into cups through a fine sieve. Stir in honey while it's hot — it dissolves instantly, so there's no need to pre-make a syrup the way a cold brew does. Finish with a small squeeze of lemon or lime if you'd like a brighter edge.
    6. Serve hot Pour into cups. Drop a fresh berry or an orange slice into each cup — pretty to look at, and it keeps gently flavouring the tea as you sip.

    Good to know

    • Which tea leaves: a robust black tea (Ceylon or Assam) is the all-rounder — malty and full-bodied, it carries the fruit like a hot fruit punch. For a brighter, more tropical cup that lets the passion fruit sing, use a green tea (sencha or jasmine green), steeped cooler and shorter. For caffeine-free, rooibos is lovely and never turns bitter, or a hibiscus–rosehip blend gives a tart, ruby tisane like the cold brews in this set.
    • Don't boil the tea. Always add the leaves off the heat — boiling or over-steeping pulls out bitter tannins, especially with green tea.
    • Other fruits worth adding: pineapple (tropical, loves passion fruit), a few pear slices (gentle body, like apple), or pomegranate seeds for a jewel-like finish. A cinnamon stick, star anise, a couple of cloves, or fresh ginger turns it into a cosy spiced fruit tea for cold weather.
    • Passion fruit seeds are edible and look beautiful floating in the cup — strain them out if you prefer it smooth.
    • Honey vs sugar: honey dissolves instantly in the hot brew and adds floral warmth; rock sugar is cleaner and more neutral. Either works — no syrup step needed here.
    • Best fresh and hot. Leftovers keep in the fridge 1–2 days; gently rewarm, or pour over ice for an impromptu iced version.
