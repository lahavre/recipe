West Lake Longjing

Pre-Qingming Dragon Well from Longjing Village — tender flat leaves brewed gently in a glass teapot. Cool water, a short steep, poured clear and re-steeped. No sugar, no fruit; just the leaf.

    Method

    1. Warm the teapot Rinse the glass teapot and cups with a little hot water and tip it out. A warm pot keeps the brew temperature steady — and the clear glass lets you watch the leaves unfurl.
    2. Cool the water Bring fresh water to a boil, then let it stand uncovered for 2–3 minutes to drop to about 80–85°C. Boiling water scalds these tender pre-Qingming leaves and turns them grassy and bitter, so this rest matters.
      Cool 3:00
    3. Add the leaves and water Put the leaves in the warm pot, then pour the cooled water down the side rather than straight onto the leaves. Fill to your batch level. The leaves will float, then slowly sink and stand upright as they open.
    4. Steep — keep it short Leave the lid off or ajar; trapped heat stews green tea. The first infusion runs about 1:30. Later infusions want a little longer — pick the preset for the round you're on.
      Steep 1:30
    5. Pour it all out When the timer's up, pour the entire pot out into the cups (or a pitcher), down to the last drops. Don't let brewed tea sit on the leaves between sips — that's what turns it bitter. The liquor should be a pale, clear jade-green.
    6. Re-steep Add fresh 80–85°C water to the same leaves for a second and third infusion, each a touch longer than the last. The second steep is often the sweetest. Good Longjing gives three rounds before it fades.

    Good to know

    • This is a premium spring green tea — the opposite of the hibiscus cold brews. Treat it gently: cool water, short steeps, no sweetener and no fruit, which would only bury its delicate chestnut-sweet aroma.
    • If your pot has a built-in strainer or a removable infuser basket, use it — lifting the leaves out entirely between rounds gives you the most control over bitterness.
    • No thermometer? Boil, then rest 2–3 minutes with the lid off. Roughly: big bubbles = too hot; small "shrimp-eye" bubbles and steam settling = about right.
    • Leaf dose is about 3 g per cup (a heaped teaspoon). Use a touch less if you like it lighter — easier than fixing an over-strong pot.
    • Drink it the day you open and brew it. Longjing is at its best fresh; store the rest of the tin sealed, cool, and away from light and strong smells.
